How to Build Things

April 14, 2026

Building something—whether it's a product, a feature, or even just a weekend project—follows a rhythm. It's not magic, and it's not as complicated as we sometimes make it. Here's how I think about it.

Start with a Clear Problem

The best projects solve a real problem. Spend time understanding what the problem actually is, not what you think it is. Talk to people who have the problem. Write it down. Make sure you're not just building something cool—make sure people actually need it.

"The most important thing is to solve a real problem for real people."

Do the Simplest Thing First

Don't start with architecture diagrams and perfect abstractions. Start with the simplest possible solution. Get it working first, even if it's messy. You'll learn a lot in the process, and often the simple solution is good enough.

The enemy of shipping is perfection. Perfection is a treadmill—there's always one more thing to polish. Ship something real, get feedback, iterate.

Build in Public

Show your work early and often. Share your progress, your struggles, your learnings. The feedback you get will be invaluable. Plus, you'll be amazed at how many people want to help once they understand what you're trying to do.

Public building also keeps you accountable. It's harder to abandon a project when people are watching and rooting for you.

Focus on Core Value

Every feature you add is maintenance debt. Every polish takes time. Focus ruthlessly on what makes your thing valuable. Cut everything else. You can always add more later, but launching with less is better than never launching at all.

Ship It

At some point, you have to ship. No amount of internal testing replaces real-world usage. Put it out there, and be ready to learn from how people actually use it.

The act of shipping is where learning accelerates. Stop planning and start building.
